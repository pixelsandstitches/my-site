The starting point

A beloved publication, ready for its next chapter

Established in 1991 by volunteer residents, the newsletter has run continuously for over three decades — a real institutional achievement for an all-volunteer publication. The goal of this redesign wasn’t to fix something broken, but to give that same content room to breathe: clearer hierarchy, more white space, and a structure that makes the newsletter as easy to pick up as it is valuable to read.

“Density of information is fine. Density of reading effort is not.” — the guiding principle behind the redesign
Editorial structure

Six sections, each with a job

Every piece of content was sorted against a clear test: does this belong in the official record, the events calendar, a neighbor's shared knowledge, a curated list, or the kids' page? Ambiguous content — like a recurring "interest" column that blurred nature writing with lifestyle tips — was deliberately merged once testing showed readers couldn't tell the two apart anyway.

01 · Always 1 page
Community News
The official record, told as a gist, not a transcript. Compact cards carry one bolded fact each, with a consistent pointer to the full record online.
02 · Flexes with the calendar
Around Beachcrest
Events, gatherings, volunteer days. The highest-energy section — real photography, date badges, organizer voice.
03 · Flexes with content
Neighbor Notes
Resident-shared knowledge, rooted in season and place — governed by an explicit sourcing standard (see below).
04 · Always 1 page
Around the Region
A tight, scannable listings grid — curated, not authored, and the design says so honestly.
05 · Always 2 pages
Junior Explorer
A standalone tear-out for young readers, built to survive being physically separated from the issue.
06 · Always 1 page
Directory & Planner
Board contacts and the full quarter calendar — the most-referenced page, placed on the back cover by design.
The governing principle
It’s fine for a page to hold a lot of information — as long as no single piece of information takes more than a glance to find.
This is what makes a dense civic page (an Annual Meeting recap, say) feel approachable rather than exhausting: pull the load-bearing fact — a date, a percentage, a deadline — out of the sentence and make it impossible to miss, even at a glance.
Before & after

Same content, clearer hierarchy

Governance content — meeting minutes, budgets, bylaw updates — carries real obligations for residents, especially around an Annual Meeting vote. The challenge wasn't trimming the content, it was finding a layout where the facts that matter most don't get lost in the facts that matter eventually.

Before · full-page articles

Governance content ran as four separate full pages: Annual Meeting notice, bylaw proposal text, a legal explainer, and a standalone FAQ — each formatted as continuous paragraph prose.

Every fact carried equal visual weight, so finding the one date or number that mattered meant reading the whole page.

After · one page, six gist cards

One page. Six compact cards, each with a bolded stat and a consistent "full details → beachcrest.org" link, plus a three-option "how to vote" strip.

Every voting-critical fact survives: the date, the quorum, what's on the ballot, and all valid ways to participate — each visible at a glance.

Sourcing standard

A real editorial policy, not just a vibe

Neighbor Notes runs on a transparent rule, written to be handed to any future contributor.

“If it's written and acknowledges there's more information out there — linking to it or giving credit — it's fine to contribute. Otherwise, it's just filling pages.”
Production logic

Pagination as a system, not a guess

Six pages are fixed every issue. Two sections — Around Beachcrest and Neighbor Notes — are allowed to flex, but only together, which keeps every issue printable at an even, saddle-stitch-friendly page count.

A tear-out, not a footnote

Junior Explorer

Beachcrest
Junior Explorer

Side one carries the masthead and a kid-pitched feature, loosely echoing whatever the adult issue covers — with light invitations back into the newsletter, never dependencies on it.

Side two is fully self-sufficient: a nature callout, a hands-on activity, a brain quiz, and a blank slot for kid-submitted art — built to survive on a fridge with zero surrounding context.
